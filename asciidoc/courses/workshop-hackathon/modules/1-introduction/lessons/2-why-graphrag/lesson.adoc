== Introduction

LLMs are powerful, but they do not know your data — and that is a problem you are about to solve.

In this lesson, you will learn why LLMs hallucinate, how RAG grounds their responses in real data, and why graphs make RAG significantly more powerful.

[.slide]
== Understanding LLM hallucination

Large Language Models (LLMs) generate text by predicting the next word in a sequence — but they have a fundamental limitation.

They are trained on data up to a fixed cutoff date, and they have no access to your private or real-time data.

When asked about something not in their training data, LLMs may *hallucinate* — generating confident but incorrect answers.

[.slide]
== Grounding responses with RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) solves this by adding a retrieval step before generation.

Instead of relying solely on what the model was trained on, RAG:

. *Understands* the user's question
. *Retrieves* relevant information from an external data source
. *Generates* a response grounded in that retrieved context

This makes responses more accurate, up-to-date, and grounded in your actual data.

[.slide]
== Seeing what plain RAG misses

Most RAG systems retrieve chunks of text using vector similarity search — finding documents that are semantically close to the question.

This works well for simple lookups but misses something important: *relationships*.

A graph database stores not just facts, but the *connections* between facts.
This is exactly what makes reasoning possible.

[.slide]
== Combining graphs and RAG

GraphRAG combines graph traversal with vector search to give LLMs richer context.

*Richer context* — graphs capture relationships between entities, so retrieval can follow connections across multiple hops.

*Improved accuracy* — combining vector similarity with graph traversal produces more precise, context-aware results.

*Explainability* — graph paths show exactly why a result was retrieved, making it easier to audit and debug.

*Flexible queries* — you can combine full-text search, vector search, and Cypher-based queries.

*Enhanced reasoning* — graphs enable multi-hop reasoning, supporting advanced use cases like recommendations, fraud detection, and knowledge discovery.

[.slide.col-2]
== Seeing GraphRAG in action

[.col]
====
Imagine a customer asks: *"What products would someone like me enjoy?"*

A vector search finds products with similar descriptions to what the customer bought.

A graph traversal finds: customers who bought the same products → what else did they buy → filter out what this customer already has.

That second query is only possible if the *connections are stored*.

== Choosing Neo4j as the GraphRAG backbone

Neo4j is a native graph database purpose-built for connected data.

It stores relationships as first-class citizens — not as foreign keys or join tables, but as direct pointers between nodes.

This makes multi-hop traversals fast and natural, which is why Neo4j is widely used as the knowledge graph layer in GraphRAG applications.
